🔍 Advanced OSINT – Corporate & Social Graph Intelligence

Corporate Hierarchy Mapping

Reconstruct org charts from LinkedIn job titles, conference bios, and news articles. Identify decision‑makers, IT staff, finance, and HR – the primary targets for impersonation. Use Maltego transforms or theHarvester to correlate email patterns with roles.

# theHarvester for email discovery
theHarvester -d target.com -b linkedin,google,crtsh -f results.html

GitHub, Jira & Cloud Leaks

Search for internal documentation, API keys, Jira tickets, and Confluence pages exposed publicly. Use truffleHog on repositories and google dorks (filetype:pdf site:target.com internal).

# GitHub dork for secrets
"target.com" filename:.npmrc_authToken

Metadata Intelligence & Geolocation

Extract EXIF data from employee photos (camera model, GPS coordinates). Document metadata reveals author usernames and software versions. Use exiftool and PhoneInfoga.

exiftool employee_selfie.jpg
# Look for GPSLatitude, GPSLongitude, Author, Software

Social Graph & Relationship Mapping

Analyze friends, family, travel patterns, and hobbies from public social media. Interaction patterns reveal trust relationships. Tools: Creepy (geolocation), Holehe (email footprint), Maigret (username search).

# Maigret: find social profiles by username
maigret johndoe123 --all-sites

🕵️ Adversary Simulation Methodology

The Professional Red‑Team SE Workflow

  1. Recon: OSINT on targets, org chart, culture, current events.
  2. Target Selection: Identify high‑value individuals (finance, IT, executives).
  3. Persona Building: Create a believable identity (recruiter, vendor, IT support) with consistent backstory.
  4. Trust Building: Initial contact via LinkedIn/email, referencing real projects or colleagues.
  5. Escalation: Leverage authority, urgency, or reciprocity to request sensitive action.
  6. Validation & Reporting: Log all interactions, provide remediation guidance.

Example: Fake IT Support Call

OSINT shows the target’s department recently migrated to a new VPN. Pretext: “Hi, this is Mark from IT. We noticed your token isn’t syncing. I’ll send a verification link.” The link is a fake login page that captures credentials. The call closes with “You’re all set!” – the victim never realises they were phished.

Adaptive Scripting & Linguistic Mirroring

Match the target’s tone, pace, and vocabulary. If the target is formal, be formal. If they use emojis, mirror them. This builds subconscious rapport. Scripts should be a framework, not a rigid text.

📧 Phishing Infrastructure – The Anatomy of a Campaign

Homoglyph Domains & Unicode Spoofing

Replace Latin characters with visually identical Unicode characters (e.g., ‘а’ Cyrillic vs ‘a’ Latin). Register gооgle.com. Tools: dnstwist, evilginx3.

dnstwist --format list target.com

SPF/DKIM/DMARC Gaps & Email Header Analysis

Missing or misconfigured DMARC allows domain spoofing. Use MXToolbox or dmarcadvisor to test. Analyse headers to trace email origin and identify impersonation.

# Check DMARC record
dig _dmarc.target.com txt

MFA Fatigue & Reverse Proxy Phishing

Overwhelm a target with push notifications until they accept (MFA bombing). Reverse proxy tools like evilginx3 capture session tokens after MFA, bypassing 2FA entirely.

Browser‑in‑the‑Browser Attacks & Credential Harvesting

Simulate a fake pop‑up browser window that looks identical to an OAuth login. The victim enters credentials, which are sent to the attacker. Detection: look for suspicious pop‑up positioning or missing browser chrome.

Phishing Kit Analysis

Reverse‑engineer phishing kits to understand attacker infrastructure. Many kits include backdoors that allow the security community to hijack stolen credentials. Tools: PhishStats, URLScan.io.

urlscan.io/search/#filename:".php" AND domain:target.com

🧠 The Psychology of Influence – Cialdini & Beyond

Cialdini’s 7 Principles of Persuasion

  • Reciprocity – Give a small gift (free report), then ask for a large favour.
  • Commitment/Consistency – Get a small ‘yes’ first, then escalate.
  • Social Proof – “All your colleagues already confirmed their account.”
  • Authority – Fake IT manager, fake CEO, fake police officer.
  • Liking – Build rapport, find common interests.
  • Scarcity – “Only 2 slots left! Act now!”
  • Unity – We’re part of the same team/family.

Cognitive Biases That Enable Attacks

Anchoring bias: the first number you see influences your decision. Confirmation bias: people believe what they want to believe. Halo effect: a single positive trait colours all judgement. Use these to lower the target’s defences.

Emotional Hijacking & Decision Fatigue

Create fear (“Your account is compromised!”) or greed (“You won a prize!”) to force a snap decision. Late‑afternoon requests are more successful because the target’s willpower is depleted.

🤖 Deepfake, AI & Modern Scam Ecosystems

Voice Cloning & CEO Fraud

An employee receives a WhatsApp voice message that sounds exactly like their CEO. The deepfake instructs them to urgently transfer $243,000 to a supplier’s new account. Real case: 2023, cloned CEO voice used for a $35M heist.

# Real‑time voice cloning tools (research only)
# Examples: Descript Overdub, ElevenLabs, Resemble AI

AI‑Generated Phishing & LLM Scams

GPT‑powered bots craft personalised phishing emails using the target’s LinkedIn profile, recent tweets, and company news. Romance scams use AI chatbots to build relationships over weeks before asking for money. Detection: generic emotional language, inconsistent details.

Fake Recruiter Ecosystems

A threat actor creates a perfect LinkedIn profile of a FAANG recruiter. They message software engineers, conduct realistic interviews over Zoom (using deepfake video), and finally ask for a “background check form” that steals SSN and banking details.

Deepfake Detection & Verification Protocols

Look for unnatural blinking, mismatched lip‑sync, or inconsistent lighting. For voice, use callback verification to a known number. For video, request a live action (e.g., wave hand) during the call.

🚪 Physical Social Engineering – The Body in the Building

Tailgating & Badge Cloning

Follow an employee through a secure door while carrying a heavy box or coffee, relying on politeness. Badges can be cloned with a Proxmark3 or Flipper Zero in seconds.

USB Drops & Rogue Devices

Leave infected USB drives in the parking lot labelled “Salary Adjustments 2026”. Curiosity gets the best of even security‑aware employees. A rogue Raspberry Pi plugged into a network port can be a persistent backdoor.

Visitor Impersonation & Shoulder Surfing

Pretend to be a vendor, a job candidate, or a delivery person. Once inside, photograph sticky notes with passwords, watch screens for sensitive data, or plant devices.

Defensive Measures

Challenge every unknown person, implement clean desk policies, use badge encryption, and install cameras at entry points. Run surprise drills.

🏢 Enterprise Social Engineering & Financial Fraud

Business Email Compromise (BEC)

An attacker impersonates a CEO or vendor and sends an email to the finance department: “Please wire $150,000 to this new account for the upcoming project.” The email looks legitimate because the attacker has compromised a real mailbox or registered a look‑alike domain.

Payroll Diversion & Invoice Fraud

Impersonate an employee and request a direct‑deposit change to a fraudster’s account. Fake invoices from known suppliers with altered bank details are another common tactic.

Helpdesk Impersonation & Password Reset Abuse

An attacker calls the IT helpdesk, claiming to be a remote worker who forgot their VPN password. With a few pieces of OSINT‑derived information (employee ID, manager’s name), they convince the agent to reset the password over the phone.

Vendor & Partner Impersonation

Attackers compromise a trusted supplier’s email and send poisoned attachments or fake invoices. They rely on the existing trust relationship to bypass suspicion.

🪪 Identity Fabrication – Building Believable Lies

Synthetic Identities & Burner Infrastructure

Create a complete fake persona: LinkedIn profile with a realistic photo (AI‑generated), a matching Twitter account, a personal website, and even a fake job history. This persona can be used for weeks or months before the attack, building credibility.

Consistency Management & Behavioral Realism

The persona must have consistent details across all platforms (education, location, writing style). They post occasionally, interact with real people, and build a social footprint. Tools: Fake Name Generator, ThisPersonDoesNotExist.com (research only).

Fake Employment Trails & Social Proof

Use a fake business registration, a basic website, and even a burner phone number to pass basic verification checks. The more layers of fake reality, the harder it is to detect.

📖 Real‑World Social Engineering Breaches Dissected

MGM Resorts (2023)

Attackers used a vishing call to the IT helpdesk, impersonating an employee found via LinkedIn. They tricked the agent into resetting the MFA token, leading to a ransomware attack that shut down slot machines and room key systems for days. Estimated loss: $100M+.

Twitter/X Employee Compromise (2020)

A teenager and accomplices called Twitter employees, pretending to be IT support, and convinced them to hand over internal tool credentials. They hijacked high‑profile accounts (Elon Musk, Barack Obama) for a Bitcoin scam. The vulnerability: human trust in an internal help desk.

Uber (2022)

The attacker used MFA fatigue – spamming an employee with push notifications until they accepted, then accessed internal systems including Slack, AWS, and vulnerability reports. The attacker’s edge: persistence and psychological pressure.

Common Thread

In every case, the vulnerability was not a firewall or an exploit – it was a person who trusted, a process that was bypassed, or a moment of fatigue that was exploited. Human hardening is the final frontier.

🧠 The Elite Social Engineer’s Mindset

“What would this person trust?”

Every target has a trust framework: their boss, their IT department, their bank. Attackers map that framework and inject themselves into it. Always ask: “Who does this person never question?”

“What process can be abused?”

Password resets, guest Wi‑Fi access, vendor onboarding – every organisation has processes that can be socially engineered. Look for the path of least resistance.

“What emotional trigger works here?”

Fear? Greed? Curiosity? Authority? Empathy? The most successful attacks match the trigger to the target’s personality and current life situation.

The Golden Rule for Defenders

Never trust an unsolicited request, no matter how convincing. Always verify through a separate, known channel. A 30‑second phone call can stop a million‑dollar breach.

🛡️ Human Risk Management & Defensive Culture

Trust Verification Protocols

Implement a policy: any request for sensitive data, payments, or credential changes must be verified through a second channel (e.g., a phone call to a known number). No exceptions, even for the CEO.

Phishing Simulation & Security Training

Run regular, realistic simulations that teach employees how to spot manipulation. Reward those who report phishing, not those who simply avoid clicking. Create a blame‑free culture where people feel safe reporting mistakes.

Executive & High‑Value Target Protection

Register look‑alike domains, monitor for deepfake content, and brief executives on voice‑cloning risks. Implement hardware MFA tokens (FIDO2) for all high‑privilege accounts.

Insider Threat Awareness & Incident Escalation

Monitor for unusual data access patterns. Train HR and legal on how to handle social engineering incidents. Have a clear escalation path: report → investigate → contain → remediate.

🧪 Human Attack Surface Labs

OSINT Challenges

Set up a fake company website and challenge yourself to find as much information as possible in 30 minutes. Reconstruct the org chart, find employee personal emails, and map the technology stack.

Phishing Detection Drills

Use open‑source phishing kits (PhishTank, URLScan.io) to analyse real phishing emails. Identify the red flags: sender address, link destination, urgency language. Build your own detection checklist.

Deepfake & AI Detection Labs

Compare real and deepfake videos using tools like Deepware or Sensity. Train your eye to spot unnatural facial movements and audio inconsistencies.

Red‑Team Simulation Exercise

With permission, run a simulated vishing campaign against your own organisation. Record the calls (with consent), analyse what worked, and present findings to leadership. This closes the loop between theory and practice.

The Sacred Rule of Labs

All exercises must be authorised, controlled, and debriefed. The goal is to strengthen the human firewall, not to shame or punish. Psychological safety is paramount.
